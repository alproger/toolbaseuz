Uzbek Script: The History of Latin and Cyrillic Alphabets, Differences and Conversion Rules

Uzbek switched to Latin script in 1993, but Cyrillic is still widely used. A detailed look at the history of both scripts, their differences, and the rules for sh, ch, o‘, g‘.

1. Brief history

The Uzbek writing system changed three times in the 20th century. Until 1929 the Arabic script was used. 1929–1940 saw a transition to Latin. Then from 1940, Soviet policy introduced the Cyrillic alphabet, which remained the sole official script until 1991.

After independence, on 2 September 1993, the Supreme Assembly (Oliy Majlis) adopted a new Latin alphabet. A slightly refined version was approved in 1995 and remains the standard used in schools and official documents today.

However, the transition is not yet complete. Cyrillic is still widely used by older generations, in the press, literature and some government agencies. That is why Latin ↔ Cyrillic conversion remains very relevant today.

2. Latin vs Cyrillic: key differences

The Uzbek Latin alphabet has 29 letters plus 2 digraphs (sh, ch) and the ng combination. The Cyrillic alphabet has 35 characters, including special letters for certain Uzbek sounds.

The most important differences:

  • O‘ (o with stroke) — Latin: o‘, Cyrillic: ў. Found in words like bo‘lmoq, ko‘rmoq, yo‘l.
  • G‘ (g with stroke) — Latin: g‘, Cyrillic: ғ. Used in g‘alla, g‘oya, g‘ildirak.
  • Sh digraph — Latin: two letters sh, Cyrillic: one letter ш.
  • Ch digraph — Latin: ch, Cyrillic: ч.
  • Ng — Latin: ng (combination), Cyrillic: нг (two letters). In tong, rang, keng.
  • Soft sign (ʼ) — Latin apostrophe: sanʼat, Cyrillic: ъ: санъат.
  • Ye/E problem — Cyrillic е is read as ye at the start of a word or after a vowel, and as e elsewhere.

4. Conversion challenges

4.1. O‘ and G‘ apostrophes

The most common mistake in Latin-to-Cyrillic conversion: o‘ (Latin) must become ў (Cyrillic), not simply о. The apostrophe in the Latin character signals a specific Uzbek sound that has its own Cyrillic letter.

4.2. The Ye/E problem

In Cyrillic, the letter е (e) is read as ye at the start of a word or after a vowel, and as e after consonants. Automated converters must know this rule for correct Latin output.

4.3. Borrowed words

Russian loan words in Uzbek often have unusual letter combinations. For example: kompyuter (Latin) ↔ компютер (Cyrillic), internet ↔ интернет.

5. Which script to use today?

  • Education: Latin is the official teaching standard in Uzbek schools.
  • Official documents: Latin is required by law in Uzbekistan.
  • Internet and social media: both Latin and Cyrillic are used; Latin is gaining ground.
  • Older generation: many still use Cyrillic, especially in print media.
  • Diaspora: Uzbek communities in Russia and Central Asian countries mostly use Cyrillic.
