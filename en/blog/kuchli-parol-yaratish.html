Creating Strong Passwords: 7 Essential Rules and Security Basics

A weak password is one of the biggest causes of cyber-attacks. Learn what makes a cryptographically strong password, how to create one and how to remember it.

1. How dangerous are weak passwords?

Millions of accounts are compromised every year — and in most cases the reason is simple: a weak password. The most-used password lists always have "123456", "password" and "qwerty" at the top.

Modern computers can check billions of combinations per second. A 6-digit numeric password can be brute-forced in under a second. 8 lowercase letters — a few minutes. But 16 characters mixing uppercase, lowercase, digits and symbols would take trillions of years.

⚠️ Warning: Many users choose passwords based on their birth year, name or phone number. All of that may be publicly visible on social media — an easy target for attackers.

2. Brute Force vs Dictionary Attack

2.1. Brute Force

Every possible combination is tried in sequence. Effective against short passwords, practically impossible against long complex ones.

  • 4-digit PIN: 10,000 combinations — almost instant
  • 6 lowercase letters: 308 million — a few seconds
  • 8 mixed characters: 6 quadrillion — several years
  • 16 mixed characters: practically impossible

2.2. Dictionary Attack

Common words, popular passwords and their variations are tested. "P@ssw0rd", "Admin123!" — these "smart" passwords are already in purpose-built word lists.

3. The 7 rules of a strong password

Rule 1: At least 16 characters

Each extra character increases complexity exponentially. 12 characters is a minimum, 16–20 is optimal, 24+ is the most secure.

Rule 2: Mix all 4 character types

  • Uppercase: A–Z
  • Lowercase: a–z
  • Digits: 0–9
  • Special characters: !@#$%^&*()_+-=[]{}|;':",<>?/

Rule 3: True randomness

The password must be genuinely random — not invented by you. The human brain is poor at creating random sequences; we always leave patterns. A cryptographic random generator is essential.

Rule 4: A unique password for every site

Reusing passwords means one breach puts all your accounts at risk. This attack is called "credential stuffing" and is extremely common.

💡 Tip: A password manager (Bitwarden, 1Password, KeePass) solves everything. It generates and stores a unique strong password for every site — you only need to remember one master password.

Rule 5: No personal information

Never use in a password: first name, surname, date of birth, phone number, city, favourite team, or pet’s name — all potentially visible on social media.

Rule 6: Never share your password

No legitimate service asks for your password by email, phone or chat. If someone does — it is a phishing attack.

Rule 7: Two-factor authentication (2FA)

Even a strong password can be stolen through phishing. With 2FA enabled, the attacker also needs your phone or authenticator app.

5. Passphrase — an alternative

A long random password is hard to memorise. A passphrase is a sequence of 4–6 unrelated words: apple-river-window-stone-cloud. 30+ characters, easy to remember, extremely hard to brute-force.

6. How to store passwords safely

  • Password manager (recommended): Bitwarden (free, open source), 1Password, KeePass
  • Browser storage (acceptable): Chrome, Firefox, Safari built-in managers — fine for single-device use
  • Paper (sometimes): safe if kept physically secure (e.g. in a safe)
  • Memorisation: only for the most critical 1–2 passwords (master password, email)
