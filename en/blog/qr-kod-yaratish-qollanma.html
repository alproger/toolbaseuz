QR Code Generation: URL, Wi-Fi, vCard — Complete Guide

What is a QR code, how does it work and where is it used? Generate URL, Wi-Fi, phone, email and vCard QR codes for free — choose your colour, add a logo and caption.

1. What is a QR code?

QR code (Quick Response) was invented in 1994 in Japan by Denso Wave, a Toyota subsidiary. Unlike a regular barcode, a QR code stores data in both horizontal and vertical directions — so it holds far more information.

Today QR codes are everywhere: on product packaging, restaurant menus, advertising banners, business cards, payment systems and even passports. A smartphone camera reads a QR code in under a second and navigates the user to the right destination.

📱 Scanning: Modern iPhones and Android devices need no separate app — the standard camera app detects QR codes automatically.

2. Technical structure

A QR code consists of several parts:

  • Finder patterns — the three large squares in the corners. The scanner uses these to determine position, size and rotation.
  • Timing pattern — defines the size of the data modules.
  • Data modules — the black-and-white cells that hold the actual data.
  • Error correction — allows the QR code to be read even when partially damaged or obscured.

5. Generating a Wi-Fi QR code

A Wi-Fi QR code lets guests connect to your network without typing the password. The code stores the network name (SSID), password and security type (WPA/WPA2 or WEP).

When a user scans the QR code, their phone connects automatically — no need to type anything.

6. Generating a vCard QR code

A vCard (Virtual Contact Card) QR code stores full contact details: name, company, phone, email, address and website. When scanned, the phone offers to save the contact in one tap.

Ideal for business cards, name badges, conference materials and LinkedIn profiles.

7. Colour and size recommendations

  • Minimum size for print: 2 × 2 cm. Smaller than this may not scan reliably.
  • Contrast: dark modules on a light background. Never use light on light — cameras struggle to read it.
  • Quiet zone: leave at least 4 modules of white space around the QR code.
  • Colour QR codes: visually attractive, but the background must still be clearly lighter than the modules.

8. Where are QR codes used?

  • Restaurants: digital menu via QR code — no physical menus needed
  • Business cards: vCard QR for instant contact saving
  • Payments: Click2Pay, Payme and similar systems use QR codes
  • Marketing: posters and flyers with a QR linking to a website or promotion
  • Events: tickets and entry passes as QR codes
  • Education: homework or reading material links in a QR code
